# AI-Native Role Archetypes

Who you hire and what roles mean once agents supply the throughput. Three
independent archetype sets so far:

**[[Jack Dorsey]]'s three** (secondhand via [[Diana Hu]]; [[Tom Blomfield]]
keeps only the first two):

- **IC** — everyone builds; prototypes, not pitch decks
- **DRI** — one person, one outcome, no hiding
- **AI founder** — leads the AI shift personally, never delegates it

**The Claude Code team's two hiring profiles** (first-party, from the team's
eng leader):

- **Creative builders with product sense** — "dreamers… passionate about
  shipping products that solve problems"
- **Deep systems experts** — e.g. hired specifically to build Claude Code on
  the Web ("run Claude everywhere")

**Anthropic's founder archetype** (first-party vendor prescription): the
[[Founder as Orchestrator]] — "much less individual contributor and much more
orchestrator of agents", with the founding pool expanded beyond engineering
backgrounds because AI dissolved the builder/ideas wall.

> [!claim] Hire for judgment and expertise, not raw throughput — the models supply throughput
> confidence: medium · status: supported
> evidence: "What I index on less… is raw throughput; the models handle that.
> The more important question is where you still need human expertise" —
> [[2026-06-03 Running an AI-native Engineering Org (Anthropic)]]. Consistent
> with Hu's one-person-does-a-team's-work framing.

> [!claim] Fixed role boundaries (engineer/PM/designer) blur in AI-native teams
> confidence: medium · status: supported
> evidence: "Our PMs code a lot now… nontraditional coders now being able to do
> more engineering, and you have engineers who take on things like content and
> design" — [[2026-06-03 Running an AI-native Engineering Org (Anthropic)]].
> First-party corroboration of the IC archetype: everyone builds.

Adjacent norms from the same source: managers start as ICs and ship before
managing (flat-team principle), and onboarding ramp time is a headline metric —
"engineers ship real code now within their first week." Context gathering also
stops being a people-question: ask Claude, not the code's author.

The three sets don't map cleanly onto each other — Dorsey's are *modes of
accountability* (who owns what), the Claude Code profiles are *hiring filters*
(what expertise stays scarce), and the founder archetype is a *role
transformation* (what happens to an existing job when agents supply the
throughput). An onboarding-archetype scheme probably needs all three axes:
what a new person owns, why they were hired, and how their role reshapes
around orchestration.
